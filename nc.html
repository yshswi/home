Success in consulting rests on two pillars: responsibilities (the hard skills) and attitude (the behaviors that distinguish great consultants from adequate ones). This guide covers both across seven MECE categories.
01
Mindset & Professional Identity

1.1 Understand What Consulting Really Is

  • Consulting firms don't "make" anything except slides, timelines, and Excel models. You are in the business of people — selling knowledge, trusted advice, and management skills.
  • You are essentially a highly skilled temporary employee of your client. Remember this with humility.
  • Very few analysts make it to partner level. That's fine — use this job to learn, grow, and position yourself for whatever leadership role you want next.
  • No academic program truly prepares you for consulting. It is learned on-the-job.
  • Your value is in being able to get things done well, but quickly, and to pivot rapidly to meet shifting requirements.

1.2 Understand What the Junior Role Is For

  • Learning the ropes critically involves formatting slides, checking Excel, creating data tables, booking flights, and making copies — because you need to familiarize yourself with the project lifecycle.
  • At the analyst/associate level, you learn about the ideal deliverable. At the consultant/manager level, the ideal project. At the principal/partner level, the ideal client relationship.
  • Your role is a safe, internally-facing way to observe and contribute to client management without being on the hook for the full project.
It is more valuable to be flexible and resilient — able to learn and pick up quickly — than to be "defect-free" but only on a small set of tasks.

1.3 Adopt the Right Attitude

  • Attitude: Be great. Bring energy, curiosity, and a strong work ethic.
  • Credibility: Earn it. Read beyond your project scope. Know key trends, main stakeholders, and form your own opinion. If caught unprepared in a client meeting, say "we'll come back to you" — never guess.
  • Detail: Know your stuff. Be ready to defend every number, assumption, and source.
  • Ownership: Do what needs to be done. No task is beneath you.
  • Problems: Bring solutions, not just complaints. "After a 4-hour search, I can't find this data. I'm going to (i) spend one more hour looking, (ii) look for proxy data, (iii) plug it into the model and check the output. What do you think?"
  • Questions: Ask them. Always.
  • X-Factor: Fun, committed, and professional — casual and dynamic, but never at the expense of being reliable, trustworthy, and diligent.

1.4 Step Into the Client's Shoes

  • Clients pay all salaries. Think about what you would want if you were hiring your firm.
  • Build credibility through a track record, not prestige.
  • Project confidence. Stress leadership and entrepreneurial experiences.
  • Every interview is potential business development. Be impressive yet approachable. Be a great listener.
  • Learn about the project context thoroughly in your first week. Read the client's strategy, annual reports, investor presentations. Set up news alerts.
  • Don't roll over when you think the client is wrong — raise it internally.

1.5 Mental Health Matters

Mental health is just as important as physical health. Talk about how you're feeling, keep active, eat and sleep well, know your triggers, do things you love, ask for help, care for others.

02
Problem Solving & Structuring

2.1 The End-to-End Process

Define the Question → Break Down the Question → Answer the Problem

  1. Use Situation-Complication-Question (SCQ) to define the problem
  2. Build issue trees or hypothesis trees to break it down
  3. Conduct research and analysis to prove/disprove hypotheses
  4. Develop a storyline from your findings
  5. Communicate findings and engage stakeholders

2.2 Problem Definition — Three Steps

Step 1: State the problem — Deconstruct using SCQ or issue tree.

Step 2: Define key questions and sub-questions — Use issue trees to identify what analysis is needed.

Step 3: Establish the defining concept — Can you reframe with a clear "killer slide" that crystallizes the client's challenge?

Resources to review:

  • Your firm's proposal and the original RFP / scope of work
  • Client discussions, website, annual reports, investor presentations
  • Team discussions and past internal work in the sector
  • Industry reports, news, research, and white papers
  • Existing frameworks and similar past projects

2.3 Situation-Complication-Question (SCQ)

ElementWhat It IsAudience Reaction
SituationNon-controversial background the audience agrees is true"Yes, I know. So what?"
ComplicationWhat has changed — a problem, opportunity, or threatOn the edge of their seats
QuestionSomething the audience cares about, forced by the complication"Tell me the answer!"

Complication drives the question type:

  • Something went wrong → "What do we do?"
  • Something could go wrong → "How do we prevent it?"
  • Multiple alternatives → "Which one do we choose?"
  • We need to do something → "How?"
The same facts can yield very different SCQs depending on where you want the analysis to go. Design an SCQ that leads the client in the desired direction.

2.4 Issue Trees vs. Hypothesis Trees

TypeDescriptionWhen to UseRisks
Issue TreeOpen-ended explorationNew problem; early in processToo much time on irrelevant issues; scope creep
Hypothesis TreeEvery branch is provable/disprovableKnown sector; limited timeWrong hypothesis → rework; accidentally exclude an area
Set a 2-hour limit for basic research, then force yourself to create a hypothesis tree — otherwise you'll get caught up in research forever.

Structure: Hypothesis → 1st-order "Why?" → 2nd-order "Why?" → Evidence needed

2.5 Common Mistakes in Logic Trees

  • Elements missing — a major branch left out
  • Elements at the wrong level — sub-issues placed as top-level or vice versa
  • Wrong priority order — most important branches buried
  • "Apples and oranges" — elements don't logically group together
  • Unclear formulation — vague labels
  • Broad issues instead of hypotheses — branches should be testable
  • Not MECE — overlapping or gaps
  • Mixed questions and issues — be consistent
  • Sub-issues don't follow from parent — logical disconnects

2.6 MECE Principle

Mutually Exclusive: Content does not overlap. Collectively Exhaustive: Together, statements fully answer the question above.

Apply the 80/20 rule: 80% of the benefit comes from 20% of the effort. MECE should drive analysis — define workstreams and guide research.

2.7 Evolving Hypotheses — The Descending Elevator Speech

  1. "Likely a demand-side or supply-side problem" (broad belief)
  2. "We think it's demand-side" (preliminary diagnosis)
  3. "Looks like a pricing issue" (refined diagnosis)
  4. "Pricing is 20-30% above competitor benchmarks" (supporting evidence)
  5. "Premium driven by cost structure — variable costs 2x industry average" (quantified analysis)
  6. "Renegotiate supplier contracts, consolidate SKUs, automate key steps" (action plan)
It is not essential — and often inadvisable — to have the issue tree "finished" at the beginning. Continuously iterate as a team.

2.8 Hypothesis-Driven Do's & Don'ts

✓ DO

  • Start with a clear problem statement
  • Ensure structure is MECE
  • Brainstorm the answer first as a team
  • Test with your manager
  • Revise with new breakthroughs
  • If stumped, make simple lists (structure later)
  • Test ideas with the client
  • Always have an answer or belief

✗ DON'T

  • Wait for more facts (now is the time)
  • Over-engineer the detail
  • Blindly stick to original answer
  • Blindly stick to a static methodology
  • Disengage from the client
  • Get too wed to initial hypotheses

2.9 Storylining / Storyboarding

Guide the storyline BEFORE conducting deep research.

  1. Understand the audience
  2. Identify objectives
  3. Think in terms of "key takeaway"
  4. State all key messages
  5. Organize into a logical, simple flow

Building Sturdy Pyramids:

Governing thought → Major support → Minor support/details. Ideas in logical order, same level of abstraction, complete sentences.

Tips:

  • Imagine what an insightful "big idea" looks like — simply stated, distinctive perspective, highlights what is at stake
  • Generate lots of supporting points before clustering
  • Build momentum: lowest-controversy answers first
  • Headlines should take a stand: "Revenue has doubled despite flat headcount" beats "Revenue and headcount overview"

2.10 Answer First vs. Answer Last

Answer FirstAnswer Last
Best forDecision-makers; impatient; strategic; risk-tolerantUnderstanding issues; skeptical; controversial; detail-oriented
AdvantageListener knows where you're goingSecures agreement step by step; conclusion seems objective
FlowAnswer → Assertions → Summary → Next StepsAssertions → Answer → Summary → Next Steps

03
Research & Analysis

3.1 Prioritize What to Research

  1. Do you need to answer it? Is it within scope?
  2. Does the answer make a difference? Prioritize high-variance questions.
  3. Do you need to prove the point? Prioritize what's critical for client acceptance.
If the rationale for a research task is unclear, ask: "How does this fit into the bigger picture?"

3.2 Desk Research — Quantitative Data

Clarify & Plan → Collect → Review

  • Track your sources (critically important)
  • Keep it simple — avoid complicated formulas; let others follow your logic
  • Keep it consistent — same units/currencies; use formulas over manual entry
  • Use proxies where direct data is unavailable
  • Ensure comparisons are "apples to apples"
  • Don't "boil the ocean"
  • Pressure-test your assumptions
Use industry-relevant credible sources (government databases, industry associations, academic research, proprietary databases, company filings). Cross-reference multiple sources.

3.3 Interviews — The Full Value Chain

Define → Database → Guides → Outreach → Interviews → Notes → Follow-up

Interview Guide:

  1. Introduction: Introduce firm, engagement purpose, interview purpose, confirm time, pause for questions
  2. Flow: Background → Current status → Future projections
  3. Questions: Long list with interviewer notes

Outreach:

  • Tailor the message — make it personal
  • Make your ask explicit and clear
  • Succinct subject line with a clear ask

Notes:

  • Keep notes as if the client may request them — clean, current, understandable out of context
  • Pull out great quotes immediately
  • Store as a living document

Follow-up:

"Thank you" email after ALL interviews. Share back findings or articles to build relationships.

3.4 Handling Difficult Interviewees

TypeHow to Manage
The RamblerTargeted questions; politely interrupt to probe; lay out remaining scope when time gets away
The PedantLet them be the expert; don't argue irrelevant points; attribute hypotheses to others
The QuestionerShare insights to jumpstart; transition to questions; know when to end
The SkepticExplain rationale with benefits; parse the reason; reassure confidentiality; explore openly
The In-a-RushOffer to reschedule; prioritize top questions; confirm a new time while on the call
One-Point-WonderGo deep on their area; repeat their point back; frame outside questions carefully

04
Communication & Deliverables

4.1 The Standard

Maximize understanding while minimizing time to comprehend. Partners receive 250+ emails per day. Be compelling, succinct, precise.

4.2 Structured Internal Communication

When sending documents:

  • Highlight problems, gaps, uncertainties and how you addressed them
  • Direct your manager on where to look and where you need input

Update email structure:

  • Lead with headline (good news / complication / next steps)
  • Be specific about time estimates
  • Include data and evidence
  • End with "Let me know if you have any questions"

For interview-heavy projects:

  • ~2-page weekly digest bolding key takeaways and action items
  • Top 3-5 bullets from every interview in an Executive Summary

Consolidate questions into one list/meeting rather than peppering your manager.

4.3 Verbal Communication

  • Prepare a two-sentence project description and two-sentence personal intro. Practice delivery.
  • In meetings: give clear, succinct answers. Don't know? Say "we'll come back to you." Don't panic, don't open your laptop, don't over-explain methodology.
  • Do a run-through before important client meetings.

4.4 Writing Principles

BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front):

Most important point in the first sentence. Simple, declarative, self-contained.

Structure: Main insight → Evidence → Evidence → Evidence

Orwell's Rules:

  • No cliché figures of speech
  • Short words over long
  • Cut every cuttable word
  • Active over passive
  • Everyday words over jargon

How to improve: Read The Economist. Every email to your mother = "client-ready." Every email to clients = "mum-ready." Use readability stats (MS Word) and writersdiet.com.

4.5 Planning a Deck

QuestionDetails
Who is the audience?Position, knowledge level, preferences, attitudes
What outcome?What should they do? What commitments do you need? Risks?
What role for the deck?Written report? Presented? Who else reads it?

4.6 Slide Design — Message

A strong slide is: Clear, Simple, Powerful, Coherent, Focused.

Headlines — most important part:

  • Weak: "Total spending" → Strong: "Total spending has nearly tripled over 5 years"
  • Weak: "Operational costs have risen" → Strong: "Operational costs have tripled in two years"

Takeaway box: Draws a second-order inference not obvious to the reader. Not every slide needs one.

4.7 Four Types of Slides

TypePurpose
NarrativeOverall logic — executive summaries, agendas, trackers
FrameworkVisualize structure; recall throughout deck for continuity
DescriptionText, schematics, process flows
DataCharts and tables (quantitative or qualitative)

Format rule: Word slides for logic/recommendations. Tables for numbers. Graphics for trends.

4.8 Choosing the Right Chart

The data is NOT the message. You choose how to interpret it. First decide the message, then the comparison, then the chart.
ComparisonKeywordsChart
Component"Part of," "share of"Stacked bar (avoid pie charts)
Item comparison"Larger/smaller than"Horizontal bar
Time series"Increasing," "decreasing"Line, column
Frequency"Most," "between X and Y"Histogram
Correlation"Related to," "varies with"Scatter/dot

4.9 Visual Design Principles

  • Information hierarchy: Font size, bold, accent colors guide the eye
  • Less is more: Remove unnecessary fills, grids, lines, boxes
  • Colors convey information: Light shades for large areas; dark for small
  • Brand consistency: Firm template colors and fonts throughout
  • No animation unless you really know what you're doing

4.10 Slide Hygiene Checklist

Content

  • Slide needed? Insightful?
  • Headline punchy & specific?
  • Takeaway adds a second inference?
  • Sources listed & consistent?
  • Sub-bullets support major bullet?
  • No abbreviations? Acronyms spelled out?
  • Spell-checked? Active voice?

Formatting

  • Uncluttered? Top-to-bottom flow?
  • Clear margins? Content centered?
  • Graphs: title, source, units, template colors?
  • Page numbers? Alignment crisp?
  • No double spaces? Consistent colors?
  • Consistent number & currency format?
  • Same headline font across deck?
  • Grayscale-tested for print?

05
Working with People

5.1 Managing Upwards

When to manage up: SOONER THAN YOU THINK. The moment you sense a constraint your manager could help remove.

Common manager types:

Ms. Detailed · Mr. Micromanager · Mr. Big Picture · Mr. Worry · Ms. Last Minute · Ms. Template Queen · Ms. Scatterbrained · Mr. Ghost

At project start:

  1. Ask others about your manager's work style
  2. Talk to your manager about preferences
  3. Be explicit about how they can help you
  4. Set up regular comms (10-min weekly, 30-min monthly feedback)
  5. Think about your role in helping them deliver

During the project:

  • Raise issues diplomatically — bring a solution, not just a problem
  • Practice difficult conversations beforehand
  • Pick your battles
"30 every 30" Rule: Schedule 30 minutes once a month to check in on your performance. You have a right and responsibility to track it.

5.2 Check-In Cadence

LevelFrequency
Consultants / Senior ConsultantsDaily
Project Leader / ManagerTwice a week minimum
Principal / PartnerEvery week or two

5.3 Managing Expectations

  • Meet client commitments 100% of the time.
  • Understand exactly what your manager asks for.
  • Double your time estimate when giving a deadline.
  • Speak up earlier rather than later. Offer solutions.
Don't wait until Monday to break bad news about Saturday's problem. Tell your manager immediately if anything will prevent meeting a deadline.

5.4 Risks vs. Issues

Risk = banana peel ahead, want to avoid falling. Issue = already slipped, time to get up. Manage both. Speak up — even to a fellow analyst.

5.5 Giving & Receiving Feedback

  1. Observation: "I noticed that you…"
  2. Effect: "It had this effect…"
  3. Pause: "Does that make sense?"
  4. Suggestion: "You'd be more effective if…"

As receiver: Ask for it, own it, schedule it. Be open. Act on it.

If no regular feedback sessions exist, proactively request them.

5.6 Coaching Styles

StyleDescription
TeachingClassroom-like; answer given
DirectingSpecific instructions; crisis mode
EncouragingPositive reinforcement
ChallengingStretching you; guided questions
FacilitatingCo-creating solutions; onus on you

5.7 Client Relationships

  • Be friendly — value each other as people, not just project boxes
  • Be friendly with admin/ops/junior staff — they are gatekeepers
  • Many contacts will be helpful in the future. Stay in touch.
  • Own client requests — don't forward to an unavailable manager

5.8 Teamwork & Humility

  • Help colleagues when you can — it pays back and forward
  • Don't undermine others; offer help instead
  • Think client data is wrong? Raise it internally first
  • Don't pursue your own hypothesis outside scope without discussing

5.9 Role Modeling

  • Observe behaviors, language, habits — take notes
  • Ask for template deliverables from past projects to see what "great" looks like
  • If a colleague is faster at something, book time to learn their method

5.10 Do's & Don'ts

✓ DO

  • Synthesize interview notes for the team
  • Review the proposal when you start
  • Read up on client strategy & competitive position
  • Ask about client dynamics & politics
  • Use bench time for proposals & training
  • Ask for great deliverable examples
  • Flag data errors with time estimates + solutions
  • Communicate leave plans early
  • Finish drafts a day before deadline
  • Get a colleague to review your writing
  • Give sensitive feedback privately
  • Help struggling colleagues

✗ DON'T

  • Copy-paste from previous projects without tailoring
  • Wait for assignments — be proactive
  • Skip optional client calls
  • Work on personal interests during billable hours
  • Forward client requests to unavailable managers
  • Refuse unfamiliar tasks — try first
  • Sit idle when manager is unavailable
  • Ignore admin requests
  • Push off reviews because feedback is uncomfortable
  • Send docs to Partner bypassing manager
  • Volunteer without scoping effort
  • Open laptop mid-meeting when stumped

06
Self-Management & Logistics

6.1 Time Management

Low UrgencyHigh Urgency
High Importance2. Plan1. Do Now
Low Importance4. Minimize / Discard3. Question Why?
  • 80/20: Know when polishing matters (final client doc) vs. when it's waste (internal file)
  • Don't reinvent the wheel — use templates & past projects
  • Focus on what the client needs, not what's interesting. Ask: "What is the 'so what?'"
  • Client-facing deliverables always take priority
  • Use your firm's network — colleagues' expertise can save hours
  • Archive work — past databases and slides become future treasure troves

6.2 Email Management

Email is not work — it's a tool to get work done.
  • 2-Minute Rule: ≤2 minutes → reply now. Otherwise → designated times.
  • Check 3-4 times per day — not constantly
  • Create templates for recurring messages
  • Triage: (a) do/delegate now, (b) later today, (c) later (flag date)
  • Clear inbox to zero every triage
  • 1-minute delay on outgoing emails to catch mistakes

6.3 Building Excel Models

Frame → Design → Build → Analyze

Four variable types:

  • Dynamic — inputs you control
  • Static — inputs taken as given
  • Intermediate — calculations en route to output
  • Outputs — the answers

Tips:

  • Start at the end — build the output page first
  • Document everything
  • Color code: Blue = hard-coded, Green = from another tab, Black = calculated
  • Disaggregate complex math into steps
  • Build automatic error checks
  • Layout: left-to-right, top-to-bottom
Do not hardcode the Excel. Make calculations traceable. Show HOW you got the answer.

6.5 Travel

  • Keep a go-bag semi-packed
  • Essentials: passport, international card, duplicate chargers, USB, adapters, photocopies of docs
  • Sign up for loyalty programs early
  • Expense legitimate business costs — if you wouldn't have bought it otherwise, expense it
  • Follow the client's hours and dress code when in front of them
  • Keep the team on time — book rooms, remind of appointments, handle logistics

6.6 Expenses, Version Control, Sourcing

  • Expenses: On time. With receipts. Right project code. Scan receipts immediately.
  • Version control: Consistent naming. Track Changes. PDF externally. Autosave on. Be the project "librarian."
  • Sources: In Word = footnotes. Slides = bottom. Excel = top of tab or comment box. Always.

6.7 Scheduling

Calendar everything with backups. "I forgot" is never acceptable for missing an appointment. Check email every 2 hours minimum.

07
Firm Contributions

7.1 Business Development

Same rigor as project work. BD performance is reflected in your review.

Proposal outline:

Partner Letter → Intro → Firm Description → Credentials → Approach → Bios/CVs → Resource Plan → Cost Estimate

Tips:

  • Reuse materials — ask for similar past proposals
  • Be directive requesting bios/CVs — share win themes + deadlines
  • Update your bio/CV after each project
  • Be ruthless about credential relevance
  • Develop 3-5 "win themes" per proposal

7.2 Knowledge Management

Work is won/lost on the quality of your case study database.

Four-Step Project Description:

  1. Big picture — why the topic matters (1-2 sentences)
  2. What you were hired to do (1 sentence)
  3. Activities (1-2 sentences — avoid lists)
  4. Results (2 sentences — quantify where possible)

Traps: The No-Show (nothing written) · The Copy/Paster (same text everywhere) · The Sloppy Speller

7.3 Internal Leadership

  • Thought leadership: Write-ups, blog posts, whitepapers
  • Operations: See an inefficiency? Fix it
  • People: Share advice, help with recruiting & training
  • Culture: Organize team activities, carry positive energy

The 10 Things — Quick Reference
  1. Step into the client's shoes.
  2. Think relentlessly about where you can add value.
  3. Learn consulting by role modeling.
  4. Always manage expectations.
  5. Prioritize your valuable time.
  6. Communicate in a highly structured manner.
  7. Provide a "safe pair of hands."
  8. Nail the logistics.
  9. Demonstrate humility and teamwork.
  10. Step up and contribute as a leader.
